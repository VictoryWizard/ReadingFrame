Biotech 2026: What Comes After a Breakthrough Year

2025 was not just another productive year for biotech. Multiple technologies reached new levels of practical impact. Here is what comes next.

By a student researcher in Bentonville, AR · January 1, 2026 · ~12 min read

In this article

  1. The Year With Clear Breakthroughs
  2. What 2026 Is Expected to Bring
  3. My Take

Key takeaways

  • Baby KJ's base-editing therapy is a completed medical intervention, not a projection; it reversed a usually fatal liver condition in a human infant.
  • Bridge recombinases can insert sequences up to roughly a million base pairs, making gene-cluster-scale insertions technically feasible.
  • Sub-four-hour genome sequencing turns DNA analysis from a research tool into a routine clinical diagnostic.
  • 2026's central challenge is not demonstrating what is technically possible but building the regulatory, financial, and ethical systems to deliver these advances broadly.

The Year With Clear Breakthroughs

For much of the last decade, biotech progress often appeared in publications and press releases without major, immediate changes in standard patient care. In 2025, several developments translated more directly into clinical reality.

The clearest example is Baby KJ, the first gene-edited infant to receive a CRISPR-based treatment designed specifically for him for a rare liver disorder. He was born with a defect that prevented his liver from clearing ammonia, a condition that is usually lethal. Physicians designed a custom base-editing therapy, delivered it using lipid nanoparticles, and reversed the disease. He is now walking, growing, and doing well; this is a completed medical intervention, not just a proposal.

On the tools side, CRISPR now has a serious alternative for certain types of edits. Bridge recombinases, enzymes originally found in bacteria, have been shown to insert very large stretches of DNA, up to around a million base pairs, where CRISPR has been less effective. This enables insertion of larger genetic elements, such as multiple genes together, rather than a single gene at a time. If these results are confirmed in human cells, they make new categories of gene and cell therapies technically feasible.

Sequencing also reached an important technical milestone. Roche sequenced and analyzed a full human genome in under four hours, setting a new record. In the 2000s, this process took months, and in the 2010s it took days. Achieving a turnaround time of hours is fast enough for whole-genome sequencing to be used as a routine diagnostic tool in hospitals rather than only in research settings.

AI-designed biology progressed as well. Tools such as RFDiffusion and AlphaFold are already being used to design new proteins, including enzymes that do not occur in nature but can perform useful reactions. In parallel, oral weight-loss drugs advanced into late-stage clinical trials, including Eli Lilly's orforglipron, where trial participants lost more than 27 pounds using a daily pill rather than injections.

What 2026 Is Expected to Bring

A likely next step is that personalized medicine expands beyond isolated examples and becomes a more regular option for certain patients. Baby KJ's case provided practical evidence that N-of-1 therapies, treatments designed for a single person, can work under real clinical conditions. In response, the FDA is developing regulatory pathways intended to make such ultra-personalized therapies faster to develop and more realistic to approve. For people with rare genetic diseases, 2026 may be the first time a custom gene therapy is a genuine, though still uncommon, treatment pathway.

AI-driven drug discovery is also moving toward direct clinical testing. AlphaFold 3 and similar models are already integrated into major pharmaceutical pipelines. They are being used to predict how candidate molecules interact with proteins, DNA, and RNA, reducing the need for some early-stage laboratory experiments. In 2026, the first group of AI-designed drug candidates is expected to enter clinical trials as companies work to demonstrate that these systems can generate effective medicines rather than only promising models.

Genome sequencing is on track to become part of standard clinical workflows. With sub-four-hour turnaround times, hospitals can diagnose certain genetic conditions quickly enough to influence decisions in intensive care and neonatal intensive care units. Newborns with unexplained symptoms can be sequenced in time to obtain actionable diagnoses before permanent harm occurs, and cancer patients can have their tumors analyzed to identify specific mutations and match them to targeted therapies. This makes genomics an active driver of treatment choices rather than background information.

CAR-T therapies, first developed for certain blood cancers, are now being tested in other disease areas. Trials are underway in autoimmune conditions such as lupus and multiple sclerosis, as well as in rare disorders like stiff person syndrome. If the results are strong and regulators grant approval, 2026 could see the first CAR-T therapy authorized for a non-cancer indication.

In metabolic disease, daily oral weight-loss pills could reach the market in 2026 if they receive regulatory approval. This includes orforglipron and several other candidates from different companies. These drugs would change how obesity, diabetes, and related metabolic disorders are treated for many patients by replacing some injectable regimens with pills.

Spatial biology is also advancing. New platforms from Illumina and other companies can measure which genes are active in individual cells while preserving the cells' exact positions in a tissue sample. In cancer, where the local environment around a cell can influence its behavior, this level of spatial detail is particularly important. At the same time, multi-omics approaches that combine genomics, proteomics, metabolomics, and other data types into unified models are becoming more common. Using multiple data layers together can reveal mechanisms of disease that would remain hidden if only one type of measurement were used.

Opinion

My Take

2025 was a genuinely different year for biotech. I tend to be cautious about calling any given year a "breakthrough year," because many past claims of that sort have not matched the underlying data. In this case, however, several concrete events stand out: Baby KJ's base-editing treatment worked in a human infant, bridge recombinases successfully inserted large stretches of DNA, and Roche sequenced a full human genome in four hours. These are completed achievements, not just goals or projections.

The most difficult issues now are not only scientific but also financial and structural. Global health funding declined in 2025, disrupting clinical trials and treatment programs in many regions. The biotech industry is still adjusting to these changes. At the same time, many of the new therapies cost hundreds of thousands of dollars per patient. Expanding access to these interventions, rather than only proving that they are technically possible, is a central problem that currently receives less effort than the research itself.

Ethical questions remain unresolved as well. As gene editing, AI-designed drugs, and highly personalized treatments become more powerful, there is an increasing gap between what is technically achievable and what societies are prepared to accept or regulate. Decisions about appropriate use, consent, equity, and long-term monitoring are still developing.

For these reasons, 2026 is likely to be defined less by new laboratory demonstrations and more by work on policy, regulation, and implementation. This includes regulatory frameworks that can accommodate N-of-1 therapies, financing models that do not exclude large groups of patients, and ethical guidelines that are specific enough to be enforceable but flexible enough to adapt as the science evolves.

If these surrounding systems are not built effectively, the breakthroughs of 2025 will remain available only to a small subset of the world's population. If they are built well, the advances from 2025 can start to function as regular components of modern medicine rather than rare exceptions.
